Kamakura (鎌倉), located in Kanagawa Prefecture just south of Tokyo, is a coastal town steeped in history, culture, and natural beauty. Once the political center of Japan during the Kamakura Shogunate (1185–1333), the city is today best known for its iconic Great Buddha, countless ancient temples and shrines, scenic hiking trails, and laid-back beach culture. Kamakura is often called the “Kyoto of Eastern Japan” for its dense concentration of historical sites and spiritual atmosphere.

Visiting Kamakura

Kamakura is surrounded by wooded hills with well-marked hiking trails connecting many of its historical sites. These trails provide not only a peaceful way to explore the area but also sweeping views and the chance to enjoy nature.

Kamakura is Best explored on foot or by local train.

Practical Information

  • Opening Hours: Hours vary by temple or shrine, typically 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM
  • Great Buddha (Kōtoku-in)
  • Hours: 8:00 AM – 5:30 PM (Mar–Sep); 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM (Oct–Feb)
  • Admission: 300 yen (entry to statue), +20 yen to enter interior
  • Address: 4-2-28 Hase, Kamakura, Kanagawa 248-0016
  • Tsurugaoka Hachimangū
  • Hours: Open daily, 5:00 AM – 9:00 PM
  • Admission: Free
  • Address: 2-1-31 Yukinoshita, Kamakura, Kanagawa 248-8588
  • Access: Kamakura is approximately one hour from central Tokyo. Take the JR Yokosuka Line from Tokyo Station or the JR Shonan-Shinjuku Line from Shinjuku Station. Kita-Kamakura Station offers access to many temples as well.

Kamakura Travel Tips

Getting Around Kamakura

  • Rent a Bicycle: Explore Kamakura’s scenic streets and trails easily by bike, ideal for visiting temples and beaches.
  • Use the Enoden Train: Take the charming Enoshima Electric Railway for picturesque coastal views and easy access to key spots.
  • Walk the Trails: Many attractions are connected by pleasant walking paths; wear comfortable shoes for temple hopping.
  • Park & Ride: If driving, park outside the city center and use public transit to avoid busy traffic and limited parking.

Cultural Etiquette

  • Respect Temple Rules: Remove shoes where required, stay quiet, and avoid taking photos where prohibited.
  • Dress Modestly: When visiting shrines and temples, opt for respectful attire covering shoulders and knees.
  • Follow Shrine Customs: Purify hands and mouth at chozuya (water basin) before entering; bow respectfully at torii gates.
  • Avoid Loud Behavior: Kamakura is a spiritual place; keep noise levels down and behave calmly in sacred areas.

Food & Dining Tips

  • Try Local Soba: Kamakura is famous for its buckwheat noodles—try cold soba for a refreshing taste.
  • Sample Shirasu Dishes: Fresh whitebait (shirasu) is a regional specialty, often served raw or grilled over rice.
  • Eat Near Komachi Street: This lively shopping street has many cafes and eateries offering seasonal snacks and sweets.
  • Reserve Weekend Meals: Popular restaurants fill up quickly on weekends—book ahead to avoid waiting.
